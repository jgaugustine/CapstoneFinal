Executive Summary

This project aims to help people understand how a camera turns light into a photo.

Most of us take pictures every day, but few know what happens inside the camera. This capstone is an interactive educational website that explains the full journey—from light hitting the lens to the final image on your screen. It combines articles (for deeper reading) and hands-on labs (where you adjust settings and see results in real time) so you can read, play, and learn at your own pace.

What is it? A web-based learning platform built around the four stages of the digital image pipeline: Light & Exposure, Sensor & Metering, Digitization & Demosaicing, and Post-processing. Each stage has guided articles and at least one interactive lab. The site is designed for photographers who want to understand their gear, students of imaging or computer vision, and anyone curious about how cameras work.

The Digital Image Pipeline

Light passes through four stages before it becomes a viewable image. Each stage answers a key question.

Light & Exposure

How do we model light?

Key ideas:
  • Photons → electrons at each photosite
  • Shutter, aperture, and ISO control how much light reaches the sensor
  • Shot noise: random photon arrival limits shadow cleanliness
  • Quantum efficiency: how well the sensor converts light to charge

Sensor & Metering

How do we convert photons → electrons → signal? How do hardware constraints shape the output?

Key ideas:
  • Color filter arrays (Bayer, X-Trans, Foveon) sample one color per pixel
  • Each photosite sees only red, green, or blue
  • Exposure value (EV) ties shutter and aperture together
  • Metering modes (matrix, spot, center-weighted) choose which areas drive exposure

Digitization & Demosaicing

How do we convert the analog signal to a digital representation? Where do errors manifest?

Key ideas:
  • Charge → digital numbers (bit depth)
  • Demosaicing algorithms fill in missing colors
  • Well capacity and read noise set dynamic range limits
  • ISO amplification makes signal and noise more visible

Post-processing

How do we store and manipulate images? How do sensor design choices constrain our edits?

Key ideas:
  • Tone mapping: linear sensor data → viewable contrast
  • Color transforms (sensor → working → output space)
  • Non-destructive editing preserves the original capture
  • Output for screen or print with appropriate color profiles

Interactive Labs

The project aims to make learning hands-on. Four labs let you adjust settings and see results in real time.

ExposureLAB

Adjust shutter, aperture, and ISO—see how each affects the image

  • Real-time exposure triangle simulator
  • Compare different metering scenarios

BitDepthVisualizer

See how many shades of gray a sensor can capture

  • Compare 8-bit vs 12-bit vs 14-bit
  • Visualize quantization and posterization

DemosaicLab

See how raw sensor data becomes full-color pixels

  • Compare demosaicing algorithms (bilinear, nearest-neighbor, etc.)
  • Upload test images or use built-in patterns

ImageLab

Explore tone mapping, color, and image compression

  • Tone curves, hue/saturation, and color transforms
  • Compression and upsampling visualization

Math Concepts

  • Photon statistics, shot noise, signal-to-noise ratio
  • Exposure value and logarithmic scales
  • Gaussian kernels for blur and center-weighted metering
  • Convolution, kernel matrices, dot products over neighborhoods
  • Gradient operators (Sobel, Prewitt, Laplacian) for edges
  • RGB as vectors, luminance as weighted combination
  • Color space transforms, HSL rotations
  • Bilinear and polynomial interpolation for demosaicing
  • Quantization, bit depth, PSNR, MSE
